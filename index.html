INTERNET MEDIA AND PRIVACY

by Jesse Browne

MEDIA ON THE INTERNET

In the modern age, more and more people are consuming media from online sources. Not only are many people getting their entertainment from online sources like YouTube, Netflix, Amazon and others, much of our news media is accessed on the internet as well. A study by the Pew Research Center shows that 38% of the Americans often get their news online, rising to 50% among 18-29 year olds. Among those who prefer to read their news rather than watch it, 59% go to online sources[1].


Almost all physical periodicals now have online equivalents, and social media sources such as Twitter, Facebook, Reddit and more are becoming main sources of news for many people. This leads to not only a blurred line between entertainment and news media, but also a single point of attack for would-be censors. With information that suggests many of these media sources were used to impact the 2016 presidential elections in the United States, it is an increasing concern in the country. However, those concerns lie in outside groups or governments manipulating the content within and directing the topic of public conversations. In response, many of these media distributors are now taking action to separate advertising from user generated content, organizations from individuals.

But who has the power to control all of them?

STATE OF ISPS


Internet service in the United States has been consolidated into an oligopoly, with only a few major corporations controlling the vast majority of the market. In the early days of the internet, ISPs simply provided a service and had little or no control over what users did with that service. Today, it is a different story. ISPs now have the ability to monitor nearly everything you do on the internet. This means not only monitoring all outgoing or incoming connections, but the content of the data being sent and received from and to those connections as well. Everything you read, the e-mails you receive, sites you visit, products you buy online, and who you talk to is potentially monitored. However, it gets worse. ISPs reportedly also have the ability to manipulate or even block that content as well. In fact, multiple ISPs have already been caught rerouting user's internet searches [2].


In 2017, the US Senate passed a bill which was subsequently approved by President Trump that allows ISPs to collect and sell any data from its users [3]. While there are many technologies that consumers can use to attempt to mask or protect their use of the internet, this creates a kind of cybersecurity arms race beween ISPs with huge sums of money at their disposal and individual customers. The only real way for the public to protect themselves online is with policy changes and a reassessment of what the internet is philosophically.

INCENTIVES

So, why would ISPs want to collect data about you? Well, first and foremost is profit. They can now sell your data to advertisers. Behavioral data can be used to serve you ads specific to your interests, even if it is simply keywords picked up from an e-mail you read or a site you visited. However, behavioral targeting is no longer used only by websites. ISP based behavioral targeting techniques are growing quickly. Both Verizon and AT&T were found to be tracking users internet usage via a technology called "supercookies" in 2014, intended to help advertisers serve their ads more intelligently [4].

As for the users themselves, about 68% of internet users said they disapprove of search engines and websites tracking their online behavior. 87% of consumers want a "Do Not Track" option in their browser to avoid marketers [5]. These kinds of technologies are also found on most phones and even operating systems, meaning you don't even have to visit a website in order for companies to monitor your behavior.

Recently, Microsoft was found to be collecting extensive amounts of data on its users. The French data protection authority Commision Nationale de l'Informatique Libertes (CNIL), noticed that Microsoft was collecting information on all apps downloaded and installed on Windows 10 computers and ordered Microsoft to cease. Microsoft agreed to CNIL's demands and 'scaled back' its data collection on Windows 10 computers. However, this points to not only the capability for this kind of complete data collection on your behavior even off the internet, but the strong financial incentive to do so and that you don't even necessarily have to agree to it [6].

GOVERNMENT PRESSURE

Aside from financial reasons, ISPs may not always have much of a choice in the matter. In fact, many government organizations have the authority to demand all data on any number of its users without a warrant and even when there is no evidence that those individuals have committed a crime. This is not something that is specific to ISPs only, but also cell phone service providers and even the manufacturers of hardware. Most major cell phone service providers currently hand all of their traffic over to NSA. As of 2013, Verizon alone was providing the phone records of millions of customers to the NSA daily [7].

In 2016, following the terrorist attacks in San Bernardino, this issue regarding privacy once again became part of the public debate when Apple refused to comply with an FBI demand to implement a so-called 'backdoor' into their iPhones that would allow the FBI to easily gain access to a Apple phones [8]. Many in opposition to the idea of this backdoor cited the potential of hackers from likewise gaining access to this backdoor. While this case in particular was regarding a person who was known to have committed a terrible crime, the potential abuse of power that this creates is an additional worry for many people.

SOCIETAL TRENDS

After the terrorist attacks on September, 11, 2001, the country underwent drastic changes in both policy and pyschology. The public was in turn more willing to sacrifice personal freedoms for the promise of safety. The government under George W. Bush, implemented many policies which would have seemed far overreaching in the past. However, even then, there was a great deal of backlash when his administration was found to be illegally wiretapping phones [9]. At the time, debate over this issue was closely tied to the highly controversial Patriot Act, also made into law by George W. Bush, that allows for the detention of terrorism suspect without trail, indefinitely.

President Barack Obama stated that the Patriot Act, "violates our fundamental notions of privacy" and "seriously jeopardizes the rights of all Americans and the ideals America stands for" [10]. However, after taking office in 2010, Obama extended several of the most controversial elements of the Patriot Act, including Section 215, which allows the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court to compel businesses - such as Verizon - to hand over customer records. In 2011, the act is renewed again and includes a so-called "lone-wolf" provision, which allows for surveillance of individuals with no known ties to a terrorist organization [10].

In 2013, leaks by Edward Snowden reveal that telephone communications records for millions of Americans "are being collected indiscriminately and in bulk - regardless of whether they are suspected of any wrongdoing" [10]. Obama responded by stating, "It's important to recognize that you cannot have 100 percent security and also then have 100 percent privacy and zero inconvenience" and "We are going to have to make some choices as a society" [10].

Recently, however, there is a rising level of distrust in both the government and corporations.

SURVEILLANCE

This is not just a domestic issue, the NSA program 'Prism' reportedly cooperates heavily with the British version of the same program 'Tempora'. In 2015, the NSA was found to be survelling German chancellor Angela Merkel and other top officals in the German government [11]. Leaked documents from the NSA also suggest the techonological sophistication of this kind of monitoring is far more extensive than previously thought.

This issue is a controversial topic. For many people, the leaks by Edward Snowden were the first they had heard of this kind of government surveillance. On one hand, many are in favor of giving the government almost unlimited power when it comes to stopping terrorism globally. However, others look at the potential for abuses of power and these kinds of agencies that exist with very little oversight, certainly have that potential.

CONCLUSION

It is clear that the internet serves an extremely important role in our society and has become considered a necessity by most. The current state of the internet has come very far from the initially very libertarian views it started with. The public's expectation of privacy has been changing rapidly in recent years and there is a considerable amount of disagreement over what it and isn't acceptable. In both the public and private sector, there is a considerable push for more and more extensive levels of data mining on all sorts of different mediums and devices. Contrary to the beliefs of some, this issue is not just about criminal activity, but what level of privacy we should expect or have a right to when using our devices or our internet service.

This debate will continue over the years to come and we will be forced to ask ourselves as a culture, what is ethically acceptable.
